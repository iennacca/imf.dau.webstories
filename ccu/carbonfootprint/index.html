Examining the carbon footprint of select digital technologies

digital advisory / imf

The carbon footprint of digital technologies is significant and poses considerable concerns to the environment.

Whenever we stream movies or search for good restaurants online, we depend on digital infrastructure whose energy consumption rivals that of sovereign countries.

In 2017, Netflix reported its users watched 140 million hours per DAY, roughly translating to 2.2TwH of data center power usage.

For comparison, that's Ireland's electricity usage - for a MONTH; equivalent to 1.5 million metric tons of CO2.

One video alone is estimated to have burned as much energy as 40,000 US homes in a year, equivalent to 367,000 tons of CO2.

Bitcoin's annual global mining operations have been estimated at 45.8 TwH, roughly 22~23 metric tons of CO2, putting it between Jordan and Sri Lanka.

Several approaches have been pursued to mitigate adverse environmental effects.

Major tech companies such as Google, Microsoft, Amazon and Alibaba have committed to renewable energy in the pursuit of carbon neutrality.

Efforts have been made in redistributing either compute loads to low impact power grids, or data storage closer to the end nodes to mitigate transmission costs.

Research is being conducted on using ambient cooling effects to further reduce energy consumption.
